Childhood Obesity in Scotland

2015 Primary 1 Children by Weight Catagory

The UK is the most overweight country in all of Europe with 63% of adults being overweight or worse. With recent research showing that once you gain weight it is hard to lose it as your body resets its natural target weight.

So it is important for long term health that we address childhood obesity to catch these problem early. In the UK 1 in 10 children aged 5 to 19 years old are obese. This is three time the level found in other European country's such as Norway.

So How dose Scotland compare? Let's look at the data gathered over the last decade for Scottish Primary 1 children (typically 5 years old) at the start of there first school year. The good news is that in contrast to the 63% of adults that are overweight in 2015 this is only 15% for Primary 1 children. This is split into 9% overweight and 6% Obese or Severally Obese (OSO).

The level of Primary 1 children being OSO has flat lined over the last 10 years ranging between 5% and 6% even though the number of Primary 1 children has doubled over that time.

Regional Variations





Using the map on the left you are able to scroll through the years from 2006 to 2015 and see how the Primary 1 obesity levels have changed across the region's.

As you can see, its not until 2011 that the survey has reach a of the regions.

From looking at the data its clear that geographic location has little impact on OSO levels. In general, the OSO level is evenly spread across the nation with OSO Primary 1 children ranging from 5% to 7%.

There are some exceptions such as Orkney which reached the highest level of 11.6% in 2013.

At the same time in 2013 East Renfrewshire reach the lowest level surveyed of 2.4%.






By far the strongest factor that influences a child's likelihood to be obese is their economic background.

Obesity by Economic Backlground


Since the economic crash of 2008 the gap between rich and poor has gotten even wider, with the most deprive seeing a 12% increase in obesity while the most well off have seen a 19% reduction.

This means that in 2015 the poorest 20% of children are 3 time more likely to be obese or severally obese than that of children from the top 20% of wealthiest families.
